SeHg Yellow Perch Poster: Spatial Analysis of Mercury and Selenium in Fish Tissue

Executive Summary

Developed a spatial environmental analysis of selenium and mercury biogeochemistry in yellow perch tissue using ArcGIS and lab chemistry data. The poster presents how Se:Hg molar ratios vary across sampling locations, highlights potential ecological risk zones, and demonstrates an integrated approach for communicating fish contamination patterns in freshwater systems.

Key Accomplishments

  • Spatial sampling analysis: Compiled yellow perch tissue concentrations from multiple lakes and mapped geographic patterns of mercury and selenium.
  • SeHg ratio evaluation: Calculated selenium-to-mercury molar ratios to assess protective nutrient relationships in fish tissue.
  • Risk visualization: Used ArcGIS to create maps showing hotspots of elevated mercury and sites with low protective selenium levels.
  • Poster communication: Designed a scientific poster summarizing methods, results, and implications for fish consumption guidance and ecosystem health.

Technical Stack

  • ArcGIS Pro for spatial analysis and map authoring
  • R for data cleaning and analysis
  • CSV and shapefile data for sampling locations and Se Hg ratios

Summary of Results

  • Length was a positive predictor of THg overall (p < 0.001, R2 = 0.28) and at 13 of 20 lakes/reservoirs.
  • Lake/reservoir classification modified the length-THg relationship (p = 0.01).
  • Ecoregion did not significantly change the THg-length relationship (NCV: p = 0.478, NEH: p = 0.662, Other: p = 0.499) or the Se-length relationship (NCV: p = 0.19, NEH: p = 0.14, Other: p = 0.27).
  • Water area-to-surface area ratio (WA:SA) positively predicted THg concentration (p = 0.03), but not Se concentration (p = 0.83) or Se:Hg ratio.
  • Water pH was not a significant predictor of THg in primary ecoregions (NCV: p = 0.90, NEH: p = 0.98, Other: p = 0.27).

Discussion

Our results support the hypothesis that length positively predicts THg concentration in yellow perch. This relationship was observed across all sites and in the majority of individual lakes and reservoirs, aligning with prior work showing strong size-mercury correlations in fish.

Length did not predict Se concentration, suggesting that Se may be less bioaccumulative in yellow perch than Hg. Species-specific differences in Se accumulation are common, and other studies have found Se trends that vary by species and ecosystem.

Because length predicted Hg but not Se, Se:Hg molar ratios declined with increasing fish size. This is consistent with the expectation that growing Hg concentrations combined with stable Se levels result in lower protective ratios in larger fish.

Reservoir classification modified the length-THg relationship, which may reflect greater Hg bioavailability in reservoirs. This agrees with previous findings that reservoir formation can elevate methylmercury production and fish Hg concentrations.

The WA:SA ratio predicted THg, supporting the idea that larger or more complex waterbodies tend to have higher fish mercury levels. In contrast, pH did not predict THg in this dataset, which may be due to incomplete pH data for many sites.

Conclusions

This study found that fish length is a reliable predictor of THg concentration in yellow perch, but not of Se concentration. As a result, Se:Hg molar ratios generally decreased with increasing length. Lakes and reservoirs showed different Hg:Se patterns, and WA:SA ratio was linked to THg concentration. pH was not a significant predictor in the sampled ecoregions. These findings add spatial context to the interactions of mercury, selenium, fish size, and waterbody characteristics in New York State yellow perch.

Impact

This project demonstrates how spatial analysis can support aquatic ecosystem assessment and fish consumption guidance. By combining chemistry data with GIS mapping, it provides insights into contaminant distributions and nutrient interactions in yellow perch populations.
