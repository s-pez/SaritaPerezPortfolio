Survival Analysis of Lung Cancer Patients

Project Overview

This project applies classical biostatistical survival analysis methods to examine factors associated with time to death among patients with advanced lung cancer. The analysis focuses on right-censored time-to-event data and demonstrates the use of Kaplan–Meier estimation, Cox proportional hazards modeling, and model diagnostics.

Data & Methods

The analysis uses the lung dataset from the survival R package, which includes demographic and clinical information on patients with advanced lung cancer. Overall survival was defined as time from study entry to death, with patients censored if death was not observed during follow-up.

Survival patterns were explored using Kaplan–Meier curves, and associations between covariates and mortality risk were evaluated using multivariable Cox proportional hazards models. Model assumptions were formally assessed using Schoenfeld residuals and graphical diagnostics.

Key Findings

  • Overall survival declined steadily over time, with substantial heterogeneity across patients.
  • Survival differed significantly by sex, with female patients exhibiting longer survival times compared to males.
  • In adjusted Cox models, male sex and worse ECOG performance status were associated with increased hazard of death.
  • Age showed evidence of a non-linear association with mortality, while weight loss and daily caloric intake were not strongly associated with survival after adjustment.
  • Model diagnostics indicated that proportional hazards assumptions were generally reasonable, with minor departures explored through sensitivity analyses.

Conclusion

This project demonstrates the application of standard survival analysis techniques commonly used in biostatistics and clinical research. Results highlight the importance of functional status and sex as predictors of mortality in lung cancer patients, while illustrating best practices in model diagnostics and sensitivity analysis.
